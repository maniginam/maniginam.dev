There is a lot of poop in my life these days, but have you ever thought about the poop of a dragon? Turns out they are as magical as you may have imagined and compiled of little bits of 1’s & 0’s.

A dragon poops each time you click on an element, proceed to drag it into or across a DOM, and finally drop it somewhere within an allowable component of said DOM.

So, these days, I am not only working on potty training Rex (as well as Leo--fingers crossed), but also my own personalized Night Fury, whom I call Epic, to both begin & end pooping where I want whichever creature I’m working with to do so.

Did you know dragons speak javascript? Well, they do. For me, this is an added challenge, because in the midst of a dragon drop, the trainer should ideally be able to witness every movement of the dragon to be sure the drop is positioned correctly. If this doesn’t occur, things could get messy and even explosive…

I’ve seen dragon diarrhea at this point… The whole screen goes blank and the browser must be refreshed--sometimes it’s even worse, so you really want to control your dragon every time he drops.

Good thing he’s cute!

Why Drag-and-Drop Matters

The thing about drag-and-drop that nobody tells you up front is that it is deceptively simple as a concept and absurdly complex as an implementation. The user just wants to grab a card and move it somewhere. That is it. But underneath, you need to track the drag start position, calculate valid drop zones, handle the visual feedback during the drag, update the data model on drop, and re-render the list -- all while making it feel smooth and not having the browser do something unexpected with its own drag behaviors.

This was one of those features where I really felt the absence of good test coverage. The browser is the only environment where you can truly see if the drag behavior is correct, which means trial-and-error development. Click, drag, see what happens, fix, repeat. It was slow, and it was the opposite of test-driven. That tension between "I should write a test first" and "I genuinely do not know what the correct output looks like yet" is one of the hardest parts of learning TDD in real applications.

I eventually found a middle ground: write the exploratory code first to understand the behavior, then go back and write the tests to lock it in. Refactoring came after that, once I could see the shape of the solution. Not the textbook Red-Green-Refactor cycle, but an honest adaptation of it for the real world of browser APIs and JavaScript event handlers that do not behave the way the documentation suggests.
