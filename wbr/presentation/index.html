What AI Actually Is

AI is autocomplete on steroids.

It reads billions of pages of text, then predicts the most likely next word. That's the magic trick.

  • Not a brain — it has no understanding
  • Not alive — no feelings or goals
  • A very fast pattern matcher trained on human writing
  • Think: a student who read everything but experienced nothing
  • 55% of adults over 50 already use AI without knowing it — predictive text, GPS, spam filters

What AI Is Good and Bad At

Good at:

  • Drafting emails, letters, posts
  • Summarizing long documents
  • Translating jargon to plain English
  • Brainstorming and planning
  • Answering questions patiently

Bad at:

  • Facts — makes things up ("hallucinations")
  • Math — surprisingly bad
  • Recent events — knowledge cutoff
  • Legal/medical advice — not a doctor
  • Your situation — doesn't know you
Golden Rule: Use AI like a smart intern — helpful and fast, but always check their work.

"Will AI take my job?"

AI won't take your job. Someone who uses AI might.

Jobs that will change

  • Data entry and repetitive paperwork
  • Basic customer service scripts
  • Simple content generation
  • Routine scheduling and admin

Jobs that are safe

  • Anything requiring hands (plumbing, electrical, welding)
  • Relationship work (sales, caregiving, teaching)
  • Creative judgment and local knowledge
  • Trust-based work (your doctor, your lawyer)

Best move? Learn AI as a tool for YOUR job — more valuable, not replaceable.

"Do I need to be technical?"

No.

If you can text a friend, you can use AI.

  • Talk to it in plain English — no coding
  • Tools you already use have AI built in (Google, Canva, QuickBooks)
  • Best prompts = clear instructions, like telling an employee what you need

The only skill you need:

  • Be specific about what you want
  • Give context (who, what, why)
  • Review the output — don't blindly trust
  • Ask follow-ups if it's not right

That's communication skill. You already have it.

17 / 30

"Will AI steal my business data?"

Free tiers — be careful

  • Free ChatGPT, Gemini may use inputs to train
  • Don't paste trade secrets, client data, or financials
  • Think of free AI like a public park — don't leave valuables

Paid tiers — usually safe

  • Paid plans typically don't train on your data
  • Enterprise plans guarantee data privacy in writing
  • Check the privacy policy — or ask AI to explain it!
Rule of thumb: If you wouldn't post it on Facebook, don't put it in free AI.

"Should I use AI to build a bunch of websites?"

The trap

AI makes it easy to generate 100 websites overnight. But Google penalizes low-quality AI content. A dozen empty sites = zero value.

The smart play

  • AI helps you build faster — but you still need a real purpose
  • Quality over quantity — one great site beats ten empty ones
  • Your expertise + AI speed = the winning formula
  • Use AI to create content, but add your real knowledge and voice

AI is a power tool, not a magic wand. Still need a carpenter behind it.

Staying Safe with AI

Never give AI:

  • Social Security numbers
  • Passwords or bank info
  • Medical records
  • Private photos of others
  • Client/customer data (on free tiers)

Safe to share:

  • General questions
  • Public information
  • Writing you want help drafting
  • Ideas you want to brainstorm
  • Anything you'd say out loud in public
Scam Alert: "AI detected a problem with your account" = scam. AI doesn't monitor your accounts. Don't click.
22 / 30

AI Voice Cloning Scams

AI can clone anyone's voice from just 20 seconds of audio.

Scammers pull clips from social media, voicemail, or video — then call you pretending to be your grandchild, your boss, or your bank.

The numbers

  • FBI: $893 million in AI fraud losses (2025)
  • 34% success rate — up from 12% in 2024
  • Average victim loses $12,500
  • Only 15% of victims report it — shame keeps them silent

Protect yourself

  • Family code word — pick a word only your family knows
  • If someone calls asking for money urgently — hang up and call them back
  • Don't trust caller ID — it can be spoofed
  • Tell your elderly relatives about this TODAY
Set up a family code word tonight. Free, takes 2 minutes, could save thousands.

Demo: "Is This Email a Scam?"

Use AI as your scam detector

You: "Is this email legitimate? 'Dear valued customer, we've detected unusual activity on your account. Click here to verify your identity immediately or your account will be suspended.'"

AI: "This is almost certainly a phishing scam. Red flags: urgency tactics ('immediately'), vague greeting ('valued customer'), threatening language ('suspended'), and a request to click a link..."

  • Paste any suspicious email, text, or letter into AI
  • AI identifies red flags you might miss
  • Works for phone scripts too — describe what they said
  • Teach your parents and grandparents this trick

"Can my kids use AI? Is it safe?"

89% of kids are already using AI. The question isn't "can they" — it's "how do we guide them."

The good

  • Patient tutor — explains anything at their level
  • Study help, essay brainstorming, test prep
  • Creative outlet — stories, art, music ideas
  • Safe space to ask "dumb" questions

The risks

  • Wrong answers that sound confident
  • Kids may stop thinking critically if they just copy
  • No built-in age filter — can discuss adult topics
  • Schools have mixed policies — check with teachers
26 / 30

Family AI Ground Rules

Ages 8–12

  • Use it together — family activity
  • "Let's ask AI about dinosaurs" then fact-check
  • Never share name, school, address, photos
  • Try: homework help, stories, "what if" questions

Ages 13–17

  • AI helps you learn, not do it for you
  • "Explain this" = good. "Write my essay" = not.
  • Check school policy — some ban, some encourage
  • Teach them to verify: "Is this actually true?"

Talk to them

  • "Show me how you use ChatGPT"
  • "What do your teachers say about AI?"
  • "Can you tell when AI wrote something?"
  • Stay curious, not scared — that protects them
